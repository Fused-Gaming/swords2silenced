# The Homelessness Narrative: How Blaming Individuals Enables Systemic Failure

## Executive Summary

For four decades, the dominant narrative about homelessness in America has shifted blame from structural and economic factors to individual pathology—mental illness, addiction, and personal failure. This narrative shift serves a critical function: it obscures why $24-37 billion in California spending made homelessness worse, and it prevents accountability for programs that fail to deliver results.

**The Core Problem:** When homelessness is framed as an individual medical/psychological problem, systemic failure becomes invisible. A $17,000-per-person-per-month program that doesn't house people isn't a scandal—it's "helping" a fundamentally broken population. Accountability becomes impossible because the problem is assumed to be unsolvable at the systemic level.

This analysis examines how the narrative was constructed, who benefits from maintaining it, and what the actual evidence shows.

---

## Part 1: The Narrative Shift – From Structural to Individual Blame

### The 1980s Turning Point: When the Frame Changed

Homelessness surged dramatically in the 1980s, but not because of deinstitutionalization. Deinstitutionalization began in the 1950s-1960s; homelessness exploded in the 1980s. The gap matters.

**What Actually Happened:**

[In 1981, the Reagan administration repealed the Mental Health Systems Act and cut federal mental health funding by 90%](https://politifact.com/factchecks/2023/apr/20/mike-pence/mike-pence-said-liberals-emptied-mental-hospitals/). Simultaneously, [federal housing budgets were slashed, eliminating over 1 million Single Resident Occupancy (SRO) units](https://politifact.com/factchecks/2023/apr/20/mike-pence/mike-pence-said-liberals-emptied-mental-hospitals/), and [SSDI recipients lost disability benefits in mass reviews](https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK519584/).

These were structural policy decisions with economic consequences.

**How It Was Reframed:**

Instead of addressing this, political leaders—particularly [New York City Mayor Edward Koch—blamed deinstitutionalization and mental illness for homelessness](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4364434/). [The media followed Koch's lead, framing homelessness as primarily a mental health question](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4364434/).

**The Research Contradiction:**

[The National Institute of Mental Health funded the first major studies of homeless populations, which found that only about 30% had serious mental illness](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4364434/). Yet despite this finding, this research "set the mold for a generation of research and policy characterizing homelessness as primarily an individual-level problem rather than a problem with the social safety net."

**The Result:**

By the 1990s, [the narrative had shifted from structural critique (housing costs, policy cuts, economic shifts) to individual blame (mental illness, addiction, "choice")](https://tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10530789.2025.2506841).

### What the Evidence Actually Shows

**Primary Cause:** [Housing costs and affordability are the leading driver of homelessness. High rent and low vacancy increase homelessness; mental illness and substance abuse do not show this correlation](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7525583/). There are zero U.S. housing markets where SSI income alone can afford safe housing.

**Mental Illness's Real Role:** [Mental illness is significantly more prevalent among homeless populations (present in roughly 21-30%), but research consistently shows it's typically a consequence of homelessness, not the primary cause](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7525583/). Homelessness itself is traumatic; homelessness causes mental illness more often than mental illness causes homelessness.

**The Causation Reversal:** [The stress, trauma, and conditions of homelessness create mental illness and substance abuse disorders](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7525583/). Treating this as "homelessness caused by mental illness" reverses the actual causal chain.

## Part 2: Who Benefits From The Narrative – The Incentive Structure

### 1. Government Agencies & Politicians

**The Function:** If homelessness is an individual problem, then government failure is invisible.
- Spending $24 billion while homelessness increases isn't a policy failure—it's evidence of how difficult the problem is
- [Lack of tracking and accountability becomes acceptable because "it's hard to measure outcomes with this population"](https://www.auditor.ca.gov/reports/2023-102-1/)
- [The California Interagency Council claims it "doesn't have funds" to track spending](https://calmatters.org/housing/homelessness/2024/04/california-homelessness-spending/)—a political choice, not a budget constraint

**Real-World Example:** [LA's Homeless Services Authority (LAHSA) used "attorney-client privilege" claims to block shelter incident reports from public access](https://www.westsidecurrent.com/news/lahsa-s-18-year-record-of-audit-failures-on-homeless-spending-laid-bare-in-federal/article_5c30a8e1-2e35-4062-8d43-2c3dd5fb2b47.html)—not because they have attorneys, but because the legal privilege claim deflects scrutiny.

### 2. Nonprofit Organizations & Service Providers

**The Perverse Incentive:** [Nonprofit funding is often tied to the number of people served, not the number of people who exit homelessness](https://medium.com/@mybuddy4u03/how-profit-and-policy-keep-the-homeless-crisis-alive-33d1f9c09ea5). The more homeless people exist, the more funding flows in.

**Documented Cases:**

- [Oakland gave a nonprofit $100 million to reduce chronic homelessness by 50% in 5 years. Instead, the chronically homeless population increased from 2,138 to 2,691](https://oaklandreport.org/p/as-homelessness-budgets-grow-why)—yet funding continued.

- [A $140 million nonprofit (HOPICS) in LA failed to pay rent, evicting residents back to the streets](https://calmatters.org/housing/homelessness/2023/12/homeless-los-angeles-displaced/)—but this failure proves the population is "hard to serve," not that the organization failed.

- [Alexander Soofer stole $23 million in homeless funds while running a nonprofit contracted with LA's homeless authority](https://www.nbclosangeles.com/news/local/los-angeles-homeless-fraud-department-of-justice-abundant-blessings/3835498/)—personal enrichment was possible precisely because the system doesn't track outcomes.

### 3. Pharmaceutical Industry

**The Narrative Function:** [If homelessness is a medical/mental health problem, the solution is treatment and medication](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10845136/).

**The Reality:**
- [Pharmaceutical companies spend more on sales/marketing than R&D](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10845136/), incentivizing them to expand the definition of mental illness
- [The American Psychiatric Association has progressively broadened mental illness definitions to include normative human distress, creating new billable diagnoses](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10845136/)
- [Medications have not comprehensively addressed homelessness; they've contributed to it by deflecting from social determinants](https://journalofethics.ama-assn.org/article/how-pharmaceuticals-mask-health-and-social-inequity/2021-07)

**The Payoff:** Treating homelessness as medical = endless treatment = endless revenue.

### 4. Legal Aid & Conflict Systems

**The Structural Problem:** [Legal aid organizations funded by the same sources as homeless services cannot represent homeless people against those same funders](https://laist.com/news/housing-homelessness/los-angeles-city-council-stay-housed-la-legal-aid-foundation-contract-vote-city-attorney).

**The LA Example:**
[The city awarded $107-177 million to the Legal Aid Foundation of Los Angeles (LAFLA) for eviction defense and homeless services, while LAFLA simultaneously sued the city repeatedly](https://laist.com/news/housing-homelessness/los-angeles-city-council-stay-housed-la-legal-aid-foundation-contract-vote-city-attorney). 

[The City Attorney objected, warning of conflict of interest: the city cannot audit an organization that's litigating against it](https://laist.com/news/housing-homelessness/los-angeles-city-council-stay-housed-la-legal-aid-foundation-contract-vote-city-attorney).

**The Gap:** A homeless person harmed by a failed nonprofit has no legal recourse because:
- Legal aid is funded by the same foundations that fund services
- Privacy/HIPAA claims block the data needed to prove harm
- Attorney-client privilege shields organizations from accountability

## Part 3: How the Narrative Enables Accountability Gaps

### The VA Example: Narrative Reinforcement in Real-Time

The Veterans Affairs repeatedly emphasizes messaging about suicidal ideation and crisis helplines. On the surface, this appears supportive. In reality, it serves a critical function: **it reinforces the narrative that the problem is individual mental state, not organizational performance**.

When you contact the VA, the constant crisis messaging drives home: "The problem is your mental health. Get help. Call 988."

What this obscures: The VA has [a documented 18-year record of audit failures on homeless spending](https://www.westsidecurrent.com/news/lahsa-s-18-year-record-of-audit-failures-on-homeless-spending-laid-bare-in-federal/article_5c30a8e1-2e35-4062-8d43-2c3dd5fb2b47.html). The failure to house veterans isn't because veterans are too mentally ill—it's because the system doesn't track outcomes or enforce accountability.

**The purpose of constant crisis messaging:** Keep focus on individual pathology. Never ask why the organization failed.

### The Three Accountability Gaps

#### 1. The Data Gap

[California spent $24 billion on homelessness but didn't track outcomes. The state agency responsible claims it "doesn't have funds" to collect data](https://calmatters.org/housing/homelessness/2024/04/california-homelessness-spending/). 

This is a choice, not a budget constraint. [Service providers use privacy and HIPAA claims to prevent data release](https://www.nola.com/news/politics/louisiana-homeless-lawsuit/article_dbd7fefe-a201-11ef-9a0c-33fb5b52abc7.html), claiming that if they had to share homeless people's information, they'd "limit their input" into official systems.

**Result:** [3 of 5 major state programs couldn't be evaluated. $9.4 billion distributed with zero outcome measurement](https://www.auditor.ca.gov/reports/2023-102-1/).

#### 2. The Legal Gap

Homeless people cannot sue service providers because legal aid is conflicted. They cannot access their own data because privacy claims block it. They have no mechanism to challenge failed programs.

#### 3. The Funding Gap

[Organizations that fail continue receiving contracts because funding is based on throughput (people served), not outcomes (people housed)](https://poverty.ucdavis.edu/policy-brief/shelter-funding-homeless-individuals-and-families-brings-tradeoffs).

**The System's Logic:**
- Service provider gets $5 million to serve 100 homeless people
- Provider serves 100 people but houses 0
- Provider claims the population is "hard to serve" (confirming the narrative)
- Provider gets renewed contract

Failure is reframed as evidence of the population's intractability.

## Part 4: What the Evidence Actually Shows Works

### Housing First: The Contrast

[Housing First programs decreased homelessness by 88% compared to treatment-first models](https://nlihc.org/resource/systematic-research-review-finds-benefits-housing-first-programs-us-outweigh-costs). [For every $1 spent, there's $1.44 in cost savings—a median benefit-to-cost ratio of 1.8:1 across studies](https://nlihc.org/resource/systematic-research-review-finds-benefits-housing-first-programs-us-outweigh-costs).

**Key Finding:** [In a four-year trial in Santa Clara County, 86% of people in supportive housing were stably housed, compared to 36% in the control group](https://nlihc.org/resource/systematic-research-review-finds-benefits-housing-first-programs-us-outweigh-costs).

**Why It Works:** 
Housing isn't a reward for sobriety or mental health treatment. It's a foundation. Once people have stable housing, they can access services that actually help. [The approach addresses the primary cause (lack of housing) rather than secondary symptoms (mental illness caused by homelessness)](https://cbpp.org/research/housing/policymakers-can-solve-homelessness-by-scaling-up-proven-solutions-rental).

### Rental Assistance & Income Support

[Rental assistance proven highly effective at both rehousing and preventing homelessness](https://cbpp.org/research/housing/policymakers-can-solve-homelessness-by-scaling-up-proven-solutions-rental). [Even one-time cash payments reduce homelessness and generate $777 per person annual savings](https://cbpp.org/research/housing/policymakers-can-solve-homelessness-by-scaling-up-proven-solutions-rental).

**Why California's $24-37 Billion Failed:**

It wasn't spent on housing. It was spent on services—mental health treatment, substance abuse counseling, case management. All necessary, but they don't solve the primary problem: lack of affordable housing.

[You cannot medicate someone into stable housing when there's no housing available](https://cbpp.org/research/housing/policymakers-can-solve-homelessness-by-scaling-up-proven-solutions-rental).

## Part 5: How The Narrative Obscures the Money Trail

### The System's Circularity

```
Narrative: "Homeless people are mentally ill/addicted and can't help themselves"
             ↓
Funding follows: Mental health services, substance abuse treatment, crisis interventions
             ↓
These programs don't reduce homelessness (because housing is the primary issue)
             ↓
Failure gets reframed: "This population is too broken to fix"
             ↓
More funding flows into the same organizations
             ↓
Narrative reinforced
```

### Where The Money Actually Goes

1. **Services that don't solve the problem** – Mental health treatment, medication, case management (all necessary but insufficient without housing)
2. **Administration & overhead** – [LA's Inside Safe program costs $17,009 per person per month, mostly on services and coordination, not housing](https://calmatters.org/housing/homelessness/2023/12/homeless-los-angeles-displaced/)
3. **Direct fraud** – [$23 million stolen by Alexander Soofer](https://www.nbclosangeles.com/news/local/los-angeles-homeless-fraud-department-of-justice-abundant-blessings/3835498/), [$50 million fraud by real estate developers](https://www.foxbusiness.com/politics/california-homeless-crisis-doj-accuses-real-estate-developers-of-50m-funding-fraud), [$319.5 million in federal funds at risk of misuse with no fraud detection systems](https://calmatters.org/housing/homelessness/2024/08/hud-hcd-audit/)
4. **Legal defense of the system** – [LAFLA's $107M contract partly funds lawsuits that prevent city enforcement](https://laist.com/news/housing/los-angeles-city-council-stay-housed-la-legal-aid-foundation-contract-vote-city-attorney)

### The Accountability Prevention

None of this triggers scrutiny because:
- The narrative frames the problem as individual incapacity
- This narrative justifies why spending doesn't produce results
- Anyone who questions the spending can be countered with: "These people are hard to help"
- Legal/privacy barriers prevent data access
- Organizations fund the legal systems that would investigate them

## Part 6: The Structural Conflicts of Interest

### The Funder-Provider-Legal Aid Triangle

```
Government/Foundations → Fund Homeless Service Providers
         ↓
Service Providers fail to deliver
         ↓
Homeless people need legal recourse
         ↓
Same Government/Foundations → Fund Legal Aid Organizations
         ↓
Legal Aid cannot represent clients against funders
         ↓
No accountability possible
```

**Real Example:**
[LAFLA received $107M from LA while simultaneously suing LA on behalf of homeless people](https://laist.com/news/housing-homelessness/los-angeles-city-council-stay-housed-la-legal-aid-foundation-contract-vote-city-attorney). This isn't a conflict the organization can resolve—it's structural. The funder controls the legal system that would oversee the provider.

### Systemic Barriers to Accessing Medical Facilities

The framing of "homeless people are too mentally ill to access legal services" obscures the real barrier: **systemic inaccessibility combined with privacy violations**.

Actual barriers:
- Medical appointments are hard to keep without stable housing
- Privacy is compromised in shelter systems
- Weather and location make it difficult to travel to lawyer offices or medical facilities
- Time constraints from managing basic survival needs prevent appointment attendance
- Communication barriers without stable phone/mailing address

**These are systemic access problems, not individual capacity problems.**

Yet the narrative frames them as: "These people are too broken to help themselves," when the accurate frame is: "The system makes it structurally impossible to access basic services while homeless."

---

## Conclusion: How the Narrative Enables the System

The shift from "homelessness is caused by structural policy failures and housing shortage" to "homelessness is caused by individual mental illness and addiction" serves a critical function: **it makes systemic failure invisible and prevents accountability**.

### What the Narrative Enables:

1. **Organizational failure becomes evidence of population intractability** – A nonprofit that serves 100 people and houses 0 isn't failing; the population is "too broken"

2. **Fraud becomes harder to detect** – When $24 billion produces more homelessness, it's explained as "the complexity of this population" rather than investigated as systemic failure

3. **Perverse funding incentives persist** – Organizations profit from throughput (people served) rather than outcomes (people housed), and nobody questions it because the population is assumed unsolvable

4. **Legal accountability becomes impossible** – Those harmed by failed services have no recourse because legal aid is conflicted, and privacy claims block the data needed to prove harm

5. **The actual solution (housing) gets deprioritized** – Money flows into treatment and services (which are necessary but insufficient) while housing shortages persist

6. **Politicians and agencies avoid responsibility** – Spending doesn't reduce homelessness, but this isn't blamed on policy failure; it's attributed to the intractability of mental illness

### The Real Story

Homelessness isn't primarily caused by mental illness. [Homelessness is caused by housing shortage and economic policy](https://cbpp.org/research/housing/policymakers-can-solve-homelessness-by-scaling-up-proven-solutions-rental). [Housing First works because it addresses the primary cause](https://nlihc.org/resource/systematic-research-review-finds-benefits-housing-first-programs-us-outweigh-costs).

The narrative that shifts blame to individuals serves the system by:
- Obscuring why $24-37 billion didn't work
- Preventing scrutiny of fraud and misuse
- Justifying why organizations can fail and retain funding
- Creating legal barriers to accountability
- Maintaining funding flows to organizations that profit from the problem's persistence

**The system works—just not for homeless people. It works for organizations, funders, and political actors who benefit from an unsolved "intractable" crisis.**
